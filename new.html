Exercize

  1. What instruments do you hear?
  2. For each frequency band, which instruments are present in that band? Which are absent?
  3. For any instruments that span multiple frequency bands, where is the instrument most present?

In chapter 4, Moylan writes about the importance of the recordist to evaluate and talk about sound in a clear and definable manner.
Audio professionals need to:

  • define what they hear,
  • understand what they hear,
  • and communicate with one another about sound

According to Moylan, common ways of speaking about sound are inadequate and rely on methods including associations with other senses (like "dark," "crisp," or "bright") which do not communicate precise and meaningful information, analogies(like "metallic" or "violin-like") which rely on external references that may not be shared, and mood connotations which are useless due to their extreme imprecision.

Meaningful communication about sound can only be accomplished through the use of common experiences or knowledge. The sound source itself, as it exists in its physical dimensions in air, is the only common experience between two or more humans. Therefore, meaningful communication about sound will not be precise and relevant without a description of the sound source itself.

This example plays the song "One and Only" by Adele. The song was chosen because the instruments sit rather nicely along the frequency boudaries that Moylan lays out for us in the text.

While working through this exercize, think about the questions posted above.
